# OPEN LOOPS

## Premise

Open Loops is not a productivity app.

It is an emotional computing experiment exploring how interfaces might support psychological closure.

Most task management systems assume that unfinished tasks stop occupying our minds only when they are completed. As a result, they reduce all forms of resolution into the same set of actions: complete, snooze, reschedule, or dismiss.

But human cognition appears to work differently.

Research suggests that specific planning can reduce the mental burden of unfinished goals even before they are completed. In other words, the mind may not require completion to let go—it may require confidence that the future has been meaningfully accounted for.

Open Loops explores this question:

> What if we designed around how the mind achieves closure, rather than around how software tracks completion?

The product is intended to feel less like a task manager and more like a mirror of mental state.

It visualizes unresolved intentions as living forms, allowing users to observe, externalize, and experiment with their own patterns of cognitive tension.

The goal is not optimization.

The goal is understanding.

---

# Core Design Question

How can interfaces help people distinguish between:

- unfinished intentions that continue to occupy attention,
- intentions that have been meaningfully planned for,
- and intentions that have truly been completed?

Can an interface reveal that these states feel psychologically different?

---

# The Human Problem

Many people experience a constant background hum of unfinished obligations:

- emails they need to send,
- conversations they need to have,
- applications they need to submit,
- errands they intend to do later.

These unfinished intentions often feel heavier than their objective importance would suggest.

Traditional productivity tools frame this as a problem of organization.

Open Loops frames it as a problem of cognitive closure.

The experience asks:

> What is currently occupying your mind?

rather than:

> What do you have to do?

---

# Psychological Foundations

## 1. The Zeigarnik Effect

Psychologist Bluma Zeigarnik observed that unfinished tasks tend to remain more cognitively accessible than completed ones.

Unfinished goals continue to occupy attention and are more likely to intrude into awareness.

This phenomenon helps explain the feeling of having "open tabs" in the mind.

Design implication:

> Unfinished intentions should feel active rather than inert.

Open loops visually embody unresolved tension.

---

## 2. Masicampo & Baumeister (2011)

Later research complicated the common interpretation of the Zeigarnik Effect.

Their studies found that:

Unfulfilled goals created intrusive thoughts and impaired cognitive performance.

However:

Forming a specific plan for how and when the goal would be pursued substantially reduced this interference.

Importantly:

Vague intentions such as "I'll deal with it later" did not provide the same relief.

Psychological closure appeared to come not only from completion, but from concrete planning.

Design implication:

> Planning and postponing are not psychologically equivalent.

The interface should distinguish between them.

---

## 3. Implementation Intentions

Research by Peter Gollwitzer suggests that implementation intentions are especially effective when structured as:

"If situation X occurs, then I will perform behavior Y."

For example:

"When I get to the library tomorrow at 4 PM, I will open my Gmail draft."

These plans shift responsibility from active working memory toward environmental cues.

Design implication:

> Planning should require specificity.

The system asks:
- When?
- Where?
- What is the first step?

rather than allowing a generic reminder.

---

## 4. Gestalt Principle of Closure

Humans naturally seek complete forms.

Incomplete shapes create perceptual tension.

Closed forms feel stable and resolved.

Design implication:

> The interface itself should embody psychological closure.

Tasks are represented as imperfect loops.

Open forms create tension.

Closing forms communicate resolution.

Users should feel the difference before consciously interpreting it.

---

## 5. Subjective Salience

Not all unfinished tasks occupy the mind equally.

A small conversation may intrude more than a large project.

Traditional productivity systems prioritize urgency, deadlines, or importance.

Open Loops instead prioritizes:

> How mentally present something feels.

Design implication:

Users rate how much a thought occupies their mind.

The visualization reflects perceived tension rather than objective priority.

---

## 6. Experience Sampling

Retrospective memory is unreliable.

People often misremember how frequently something occupied their thoughts.

Experience sampling captures experiences closer to when they occur.

Design implication:

The product asks:

> "Did this enter your mind uninvited today?"

This creates a lightweight self-experiment.

Users can discover which forms of resolution actually reduce mental intrusion.

---

# Design Philosophy

Open Loops is not trying to maximize productivity.

It is not designed to increase engagement.

It is not meant to gamify completion.

Instead, it explores:

- emotional computing,
- reflective design,
- personal informatics,
- embodied cognition,
- and the invisible texture of thought.

The interface should feel contemplative rather than efficient.

The emotional tone is:

- calm,
- honest,
- restrained,
- slightly poetic.

The desired outcome is not:

"I completed more tasks."

The desired outcome is:

> "I understand how my mind lets go."

---

# Product Principles

DO:

- Prioritize psychological honesty over efficiency.
- Externalize mental tension.
- Make different forms of closure feel different.
- Use motion to communicate emotional state.
- Encourage reflection and curiosity.
- Preserve ambiguity and subtlety.

DO NOT:

- Build a traditional to-do list.
- Use streaks, points, badges, or gamification.
- Reward engagement for its own sake.
- Introduce productivity dashboards.
- Use bright celebratory feedback.
- Reduce the experience to task optimization.

Whenever making design decisions, ask:

> Does this help users understand the nature of closure?

If not, remove it.

---

# Success Criteria

A successful version of Open Loops causes users to notice something true about themselves.

For example:

- "Planning actually quiets my mind more than I expected."
- "I say 'later' far more often than I realize."
- "Some thoughts stay open regardless of planning."
- "Completion isn't the only form of relief."

The product succeeds not when users become more productive,

but when they become more aware of how unresolved intentions shape their inner experience.
